The primal blueprint eating philosphy

Insulin

Insulin is vital for survival. Cell receptors use insulin as a key to unlock pores with the membrane of each cell. It's an elegant way to eliminate excess glucose from the bloodstream (which is highly toxic) which is then stored as glycogenL2 (glycogen is used as fuel) for a later date.

Excess Insulin Is Bad

  • Muscles and liver are not able to store high amounts of glycogen.
  • Excess glucose not used in "real-time" (i.e. during exercise) get converted into triglycerides and stored in fat cells.
  • High blood insulin level \(\implies\) the fat cells that store excess glucose also stores the fat of of your most recent meal.
  • Excess insulin is pro-inflammatory.
  • High insulin levels leads to insulin resistance:
    • Cell receptors become desensitized to insulin.
  • Moderate insulin levels leads to insulin sensitivity:
    • Liver and muscles are more effective at absorbing nutrients transported by insulin.

Excess Insulin And Heart Disease

  • Insulin promotes platelet adhesiveness (sticky platelets clot more easily).
  • Insulin promotes the conversion of macrophages (a type of white blood cell) into foam cells. These are the cells that fill with cholesterol and accumulate in arterial walls.
  • Insulin reduces blood levels of nitric oxide (a compound that relaxes the arterial wall lining) causing arteries to become more rigid. This increases blood pressure.

Insulin Resistant Consequences

  1. More glucose stays in blood. This is bad, glucose is toxic.
  2. More insulin is produced as a result of the elevated levels of glucose in the blood.
  3. Fat cells can't release their stored energy into the bloodstream since insulin keeps the "fat locked inside"
  4. Fat cells get bigger (i.e. you gain weight).

Excercise And Diet

  • Excercise creates insulin sensitivity:
    • Exercise empties muscles and liver of glyocgen.
    • Insulin will then transport nutrients to liver and muscles instead of going to fat.

Cholesterol

Convential wisdom says you must lower your cholesterol intake. This is not true: The Framingham Heart StudyL3 totally refutes this. Study director Dr William Catelli1 summarized the issue:

Serum cholesterol is not a strong risk factor for heart disease

Study's highlights:

  • There is no correlation between dietary cholesterol and blood cholesterol levels.
  • Framingham residents who ate the most cholesterol, saturated fats and total calories weighed the least and were the most physically active.

Lipoproteins

Cholesterol is fat-soluble (i.e. it does not disolve in water), so in order to travel in the "water blood environment", it carried by special particles called lipoproteinsL5.

3 Important Lipoproteins

  1. VLDL - Very low density lipoproteins
  2. LDL - Low density lipoproteins
  3. HDL - High density lipoproteins <= good cholesterol

VLDL

  • VLDL are manufactured in the liver in the presence of high levels of triglycerides.
    • Therefore they comprise 80% triglycerides and very little cholesterol
  • Once triglycerides have been deposited, their size decreases and they convert into on of two types of LDL carrying mostly cholesterol:
    1. Large fluffy LDLs
    2. Small dense LSLs

LDL

  • Large fluffly "boyant" LDL are generally harmless (even at high levels).
  • Small dense LDL are extremely dangerous and cause heart disease
    • Small dense LDLs result when there are excessive triglycerides in the blood,

Small dense LDL result from high levels of carbs:

$$ \text{Carbs }\rightarrow\text{ Insulin }\rightarrow\text{ Triglycerides }\rightarrow\text{ VDL }\rightarrow\text{ Small Dense LDLs} $$

HDL

  • Good cholesterol
  • Cleans up any damages or oxidized cholesterol
  • Removes small dense LDLs

Increase HDL by exercising and consuming saturated fat.

Oxidation

Lipoproteins, if oxidized become damaged and are dangerous. Oxidation can happen for multiple reasons:

  • High intake of unstable polyunsaturated fats (PUFAs) from vegetable oils.
  • Reduction of natural antioxidants.
  • Low level of HDL.
  • Small dense LDLs do not bind as easily to the LDL receptors on muscles and fat cells.

AtherosclerosisL6 occurs in the arteries and not the veins because venous blood has little oxygen.

Oxidation and inflammation has little to do with total cholesterol (or even total LDL cholesterol) and is instead the result of the oxidation of a small fraction of the total amount of LDL - the small dense LDL particles.

Blood Tests

Most common blood tests do not distinguish between the benign "fluffy" LDL (sometimes called pattern A) and the small dense particles (sometimes called pattern B).

What to measure in a blood test

  1. C-Reactive Protein (Hs-CRP) - produced as an immune system response. High levels associated with increased risk of heart attack.
  2. Lp2A - Key inflammation marker associated with small dense LDL
  3. A1c (Estimated average glucose) - Measures how much glucose is attached to hemoglobin molecule. Much better than common blood glucose reading.
  4. Fasting Blood Insulin Levels - High fasting insulin levels are indicitive of prediabetic conditions.

Macro Nutrients

There are three major macro nutrients:

  1. Protein
  2. Carbohydrate
  3. Fat

Protein

Optimal protein intake is 0.5 to 1.0 gram of protein per pound of lean body mass per day:

Calculate Lean Body Mass

  1. First, determine total weight and percentage of body fat.
  2. Multiply total weight by percentage of body fat to get your fat weight.
  3. Subtract fat weight from total body weight to get lean body mass.

Eating protein:

  • If exercising and a no low carb diet2, protein will go towards repair or building cell walls.
  • If there is not much insulin in the blood, the protein cannot easily be stored as fat and so the body is forced to raise the metabolic rate and increasing ketone production.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates control insulin; insulin controls fat storage

  • Only used for fuel.
  • All forms of carbs are eventually converted to glucose:
  • A limited amount of glucose will be stored as glycogen in muscles in liver.
  • Most glucose gets converted to fatty acids and stored in fat cells.
  • It's the job of insulin to take glucose out of the blood stream (glucose is toxic).

Unless one depletes lots of glycogen every day, there is no reason to consume high levels of carbs: * Carbs are not essential in our diets (proteins and fats are essential).

Daily Carb Intake

  • 0-50 grams: Ketosis and accelerated fat burning.
  • 50-100 grams: Primal sweetspot = effortless weight loss.
  • 100-150 grams: Primal blueprint maintenance range.
  • 150-300 grams: Steady insiduous weight gain.
  • 300 grams and more: Danger zone.

Fat

Consuming ample healthy fats is good] (this goes against conventional wisdom):

  • Ingesting fats makes you feel full (unlike carbs).
  • Fats have little or no impact on glucose levels (and therefore insulin)
    • Fats takes far longer than carbs to metabolize => deeper longer lasting satisfaction
  • The nurses health studyL12 concluded that total fat intake is not statistically correlated to heart disease.

Good Fats

Saturated and Omega 3 fats are healthy fats:

  • Omega 3L13 - found in fishy oils. The optimum ratio between Omega-6:Omega-3 is out of balance
    • Decrease Omega-6, increase Omega-3. The ratio should ideally be 2:1
  • Saturated FatsL14 - found in red meat.

Bad Fats

  • Omega 6L15 - fats in processed foods (omega 6 is needed to live, but ratio between omega 3 is scewed in modern diets).
  • Partially hydrogenated and trans fatsL16 - made by heating and chemically treating vegetable/seed oils to become solid. These fats are evil - avoid at all costs.

Carbs Make Fats Look Bad

Fats are calorically dense. If eaten with carbs, the insulin from the carbs will result in making one fat.

Ketones - The Fourth Fuel

Ketones3 are a by product of the fatty acid fuel used by the liver to extract glucose from proteins. It is an energy rich form of fuel that is both safe and desirable to use.

  • Ketones can't be stored like fat or glycogen - they just circulate the blood stream where they can be used by any cell.
  • Ketosis is the scientific name for when ketones accumulate in the body and can be used for energy.
